What a Nonpayment Proceeding Is

A nonpayment proceeding is a summary process case typically brought in Housing Court. The landlord’s core allegation is that rent (or “use and occupancy”) is unpaid. While these matters are often treated as routine, they are frequently decided on details: whether the rent demand and predicate notices were proper (where required), whether the rent ledger is accurate, whether the tenant has defenses, and whether the parties can reach a stipulation that protects their interests.

For landlords, the goals are usually (1) recovery of arrears, (2) establishing an enforceable payment plan or stipulation, and sometimes (3) possession if nonpayment continues. For tenants, the focus is often on verifying the amounts claimed, asserting defenses, and protecting tenancy rights while resolving arrears on workable terms.

Key Steps in the Process (Plain-English)

  1. Review the papers: Petition, notice of petition, affidavits of service, rent ledger, and any rent demand or notices.
  2. Initial appearance: A first court date may involve scheduling, settlement discussions, and setting deadlines for motions or discovery.
  3. Settlement or litigation: Many cases resolve by stipulation. Others require motion practice, hearings, or trial.
  4. Judgment and enforcement: If a judgment is entered, the question becomes how it is satisfied and how to avoid adverse consequences (including eviction).

Because procedure matters, saving every notice and envelope (and noting dates received/served) can be important evidence.

Common Issues and Defenses

Defenses vary depending on the facts, the type of building, and the lease. Common issues include:

  • Incorrect rent ledger: Payments misapplied, credits missed, or improper fees included.
  • Warranty of habitability: Serious conditions may support abatements or defenses (fact-specific).
  • Improper service / jurisdiction: Defects in service of the notice of petition or petition can be case-dispositive.
  • Rent stabilization / regulatory issues: Where applicable, compliance and rent history may be critical.
  • Partial payment / tender: Payment timing and documentation often matter.

The firm’s approach is to identify the strongest pressure points early: documentary proof gaps, procedural defects, and leverage for a workable resolution.

What Landlords Need to Prove

A landlord typically needs to prove the agreement governing rent (lease and riders), the amounts due (ledger), and compliance with required procedure. Even when rent is owed, sloppy documentation or notice/service problems can weaken a case or change its value in settlement.

  • Lease, renewal(s), and riders
  • Accurate rent ledger and payment history
  • Evidence supporting charges beyond base rent (if claimed)
  • Proper service documentation

What Tenants Should Gather

Tenants can strengthen their position by organizing:

  • Receipts, bank statements, money order stubs, and proof of payment
  • Lease documents and renewal history
  • Photos/videos of conditions and communications about repairs
  • All court papers and notices (with dates received)

Practical tip: Create a timeline: when notices were received, when payments were made, and what communications occurred.

Strategy: Settlement, Motions, and Trial Readiness

Many nonpayment cases settle, but settlement should be structured carefully. Stipulations often include payment schedules, conditions, and default provisions. The firm evaluates settlement terms like any contract—what happens if a payment is late, what fees are added, and whether a tenant can preserve defenses if problems recur.

Where appropriate, the firm uses motion practice to narrow issues or resolve the case. Even when the goal is settlement, being prepared to litigate often improves results.
