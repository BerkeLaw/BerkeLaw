What “Eviction Defense” Usually Means

Eviction defense is not one single type of case—it is the set of strategies used to protect a tenant’s rights and housing stability in Housing Court (and sometimes Supreme Court). Defense work may involve a nonpayment proceeding, holdover proceeding, or a related motion (e.g., to vacate a default or to stay enforcement).

Immediate Steps If You’ve Been Served

  1. Do not ignore the court date. Missing appearances can lead to default judgments.
  2. Gather documents: lease, renewals, rent receipts, notices, photos, and communications.
  3. Write a timeline: key dates for notice receipt, payments, repairs, and communications.
  4. Confirm service details: how papers were delivered, to whom, and when.

Practical tip: If you received papers by “nail and mail” or substituted service, note exactly where they were posted and when you received the mailing.

Common Defense Themes

  • Service/jurisdiction defenses: improper service can undermine jurisdiction.
  • Notice defects: predicate notice problems can be case-dispositive.
  • Disputed arrears: incorrect ledgers, missing credits, improper charges.
  • Condition defenses: habitability issues may support abatements (fact-specific).
  • Regulatory protections: where applicable, rent stabilization and related requirements.

The firm assesses which defenses are strongest and how to use them: for dismissal, leverage in settlement, or to narrow the dispute for hearing/trial.

Negotiating Stipulations Safely

Many eviction cases resolve by stipulation. However, stipulations can include strict deadlines and default provisions. The firm focuses on making sure stipulations are realistic and protective:

  • Clear payment schedules and grace periods where appropriate
  • Defined repair obligations and inspection language (if conditions are at issue)
  • Reasonable default provisions and notice requirements
  • Preservation of defenses when needed

Motions, Hearings, and Trials

Some matters require motion practice or a hearing/trial. The firm prepares cases with an evidence-first approach: documents, credible witnesses, and a coherent narrative. When the goal is settlement, being prepared to litigate often improves negotiating leverage.
